# Chapter 8: Space Capabilities — Small Satellites, Situational Awareness, and Communications Integration

> **Key Thesis**: Space assets have been elevated from a supporting role to critical infrastructure in the modern security environment. Taiwan must develop an indigenous small satellite constellation, space situational awareness capability, and deeply integrate space-based communications into the national communications resilience architecture.

---

## 8.1 Strategic Importance of the Space Domain

### Background

Space has become the fourth operational domain of modern national security — following land, sea, and air. Satellite systems provide capabilities spanning communications, navigation, intelligence surveillance and reconnaissance (ISR), and early warning, profoundly shaping a nation's situational awareness and decision-making capacity.

The role of space assets in national security can be understood across the following dimensions:

| Functional Category | Specific Capabilities | Security Implications |
|--------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Communications | Military and government satellite communications | Continuity of the command and control chain |
| Navigation and positioning | GPS/GNSS signals | Precision-guided weapons, force positioning |
| Intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance | Optical and radar satellite imagery | Detection of adversary military deployments |
| Electronic intelligence | Signals intelligence collection | Analysis of adversary communications and radar signatures |
| Meteorological observation | Weather and sea state monitoring | Military operations planning |
| Early warning | Ballistic missile launch detection | Strategic warning time |

The global space security environment is undergoing the following trends:

1. **Space weaponization**: Multiple nations are developing anti-satellite (ASAT) capabilities, including kinetic interceptors, co-orbital attacks, electronic jamming, and laser weapons.
2. **Commercial space explosion**: Rapidly declining launch costs and maturing small satellite technology are enabling small and medium-sized nations to acquire space capabilities.
3. **Increasing space debris**: Orbital debris continues to accumulate, threatening the safe operation of space assets.
4. **Gray zone operations**: Approach, surveillance, and interference activities in space are growing more frequent — falling below the threshold of armed conflict yet constituting a security threat.

### Taiwan's Strategic Position

For Taiwan, the importance of space capabilities is particularly acute:

- **Island geography**: Situational awareness in the waters surrounding Taiwan is highly dependent on airborne and space-based platforms. Satellites provide continuous monitoring capability unconstrained by weather or political restrictions.
- **Communications resilience**: As analyzed in Chapter 5, the vulnerability of submarine cables makes satellite communications an indispensable backup layer.
- **Early warning capability**: Facing missile threats, a space-based early warning system can provide invaluable reaction time.
- **Asymmetric advantage**: Compared with conventional military equipment, space asset development can provide Taiwan with asymmetric intelligence and communications advantages.

## 8.2 Taiwan's Existing Space Assets

### FORMOSAT Satellite Program

Taiwan's space development has centered on the FORMOSAT satellite series, with the following history:

| Satellite | Launch Year | Orbit | Primary Mission | Status |
|-----------|------------|-------|-----------------|--------|
| FORMOSAT-1 | 1999 | LEO (600 km) | Scientific experiments, ocean observation | Decommissioned |
| FORMOSAT-2 | 2004 | Sun-synchronous orbit (891 km) | Remote sensing imagery (2m resolution) | Decommissioned |
| FORMOSAT-3 (6 satellites) | 2006 | LEO (700-800 km) | GNSS radio occultation meteorological observation | Decommissioned |
| FORMOSAT-5 | 2017 | Sun-synchronous orbit (720 km) | Remote sensing imagery (2m panchromatic, 4m multispectral) | Operational |
| FORMOSAT-7 (6 satellites) | 2019 | LEO (520-550 km) | GNSS radio occultation meteorological observation | Operational |

### Taiwan Space Agency (TASA)

The Taiwan Space Agency (TASA) was reorganized as an administrative legal entity in 2023, marking a new phase in Taiwan's space development. TASA's core missions include:

1. **Satellite development**: Indigenous design and manufacturing of satellites
2. **Space science research**: Space environment and science research
3. **Space technology development**: R&D and validation of critical space technologies
4. **Industry promotion**: Driving the development of Taiwan's space industry

**Third National Space Technology Development Long-Term Plan (2019-2028)**

This plan has a budget of approximately NT$25.1 billion and includes the following principal objectives:

- Develop high-resolution optical remote sensing satellites
- Develop synthetic aperture radar (SAR) satellites
- Establish indigenous small satellite design and manufacturing capability
- Cultivate space technology talent and industry

### Current Capability Assessment

| Dimension | Current Status | Assessment |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Satellite design and manufacturing | Possesses indigenous capability for small/medium satellites | Moderate — some critical components still require imports |
| Launch capability | No indigenous launch capability; outsourced to foreign providers | Insufficient — completely dependent on external services |
| Ground control | Possesses satellite operations center | Adequate — can support current satellite count |
| Remote sensing image processing | Possesses image processing and analysis capability | Moderate — room for improvement in resolution and timeliness |
| Space industry chain | Nascent, primarily component manufacturing | Developing — requires continued cultivation |
| International cooperation | Collaboration with the United States, multiple European nations | Good — but requires deepening |

## 8.3 Small Satellite Constellation Development

### Strategic Concept

Establishing an indigenous small satellite constellation represents the most pragmatic pathway for enhancing Taiwan's space capabilities. Advances in small satellite technology (generally defined as spacecraft weighing less than 500 kg) and declining costs have made constellations an affordable space solution for small and medium-sized nations.

**Advantages of Constellation Architecture**

Compared with a single large satellite, small satellite constellations offer the following advantages:

| Characteristic | Single Large Satellite | Small Satellite Constellation |
|---------------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Development cost | Very high (billions of USD) | Moderate (phased investment) |
| Development cycle | 5-10 years | 2-4 years |
| Coverage frequency | Low (long revisit period for a given orbit) | High (multiple satellites providing alternating coverage) |
| Survivability | Low (single-point failure eliminates capability) | High (partial satellite loss; system remains operational) |
| Technology refresh | Slow (difficult to upgrade after launch) | Fast (new-generation satellites can be deployed in batches) |
| Replacement speed | Very slow (requires remanufacture and relaunch) | Fast (spare satellites can be pre-positioned) |

### Recommended Constellation Architecture

Taiwan's indigenous small satellite constellation should be organized into the following functional clusters:

**Cluster 1: Optical Remote Sensing Constellation**

- **Number of satellites**: 6-12
- **Orbit**: Sun-synchronous orbit, 500-700 km
- **Payload**: High-resolution optical cameras (target: sub-1m resolution)
- **Function**: Daytime visible-light imagery, maritime and terrestrial monitoring
- **Revisit time target**: 2-4 hours for waters surrounding Taiwan

**Cluster 2: Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) Constellation**

- **Number of satellites**: 4-6
- **Orbit**: Sun-synchronous orbit, 500-600 km
- **Payload**: X-band or C-band SAR
- **Function**: All-weather, day/night monitoring (unaffected by cloud cover)
- **Revisit time target**: 4-8 hours for waters surrounding Taiwan

**Cluster 3: Electronic Intelligence (ELINT) Constellation**

- **Number of satellites**: 4-8
- **Orbit**: LEO, 400-600 km
- **Payload**: RF receivers, signal processors
- **Function**: Detection and geolocation of radar, communications, and other RF emitters
- **Intelligence value**: Tracking warships, air defense systems, communications nodes

**Cluster 4: Communications Satellites**

- **Number of satellites**: Scalable based on bandwidth requirements
- **Orbit**: LEO, 300-500 km
- **Function**: Secure government and military communications backup
- **Linkage to Chapter 5**: Integrated into the national multi-layer communications resilience architecture

### Development Roadmap

| Phase | Timeline | Objective | Milestones |
|-------|----------|-----------|------------|
| Phase 1 | 1-3 years | Technology validation | Launch 2-3 technology demonstration satellites |
| Phase 2 | 3-5 years | Initial operational capability | 2-4 optical and SAR satellites operational |
| Phase 3 | 5-8 years | Full operational capability | All four clusters achieve baseline deployment |
| Phase 4 | 8-12 years | Continuous evolution | Ongoing satellite replacement and upgrades |

### Industrial Spillover Effects

The small satellite constellation program can catalyze the transformation of Taiwan's existing industrial strengths:

- **Semiconductor industry**: Space-grade chips (radiation-hardened design)
- **Precision machinery**: Satellite structural components, optical lenses
- **Communications industry**: Satellite communications payloads and ground terminals
- **Information technology**: Satellite data processing, AI-based image analysis
- **Materials science**: Space-grade materials and thermal control systems

## 8.4 Space Situational Awareness

### Concept Definition

Space Situational Awareness (SSA) refers to the ability to track, identify, and assess orbital objects — including operational satellites, space debris, and potentially threatening objects. SSA is the foundation of space security: without knowing what is happening in space, it is impossible to protect space assets.

### Taiwan's Current SSA Posture

Taiwan's current SSA capabilities rely primarily on:

1. **Publicly available data** from the U.S. Space Surveillance Network (Space-Track.org)
2. **Small telescope facilities** for academic research purposes
3. **Limited** radar tracking capability

This dependency structure means Taiwan lacks autonomy in space situational awareness, with the timeliness, completeness, and prioritization of information subject to external sources.

### SSA Capability Development Plan

**Optical Tracking Network**

| Facility Type | Recommended Quantity | Deployment Location | Function |
|--------------|---------------------|--------------------|----|
| Wide-field survey telescopes | 2-3 | High-altitude sites on Taiwan proper (e.g., Lulin Mountain) | Detection of new objects, orbital anomalies |
| Precision tracking telescopes | 1-2 | Main island and outlying islands | Precise orbit determination, object characterization |
| Cooperative network nodes | Several | Friendly nations | Extended observation coverage |

**Radar Tracking Systems**

- Phased array radar can provide all-weather (cloud-independent) tracking capability
- Data integration with existing air defense radar systems can enhance efficiency
- Substantial investment required; international cost-sharing cooperative models are recommended

**Data Processing and Analysis**

- Establish a Space Situational Awareness Center integrating all sensor data
- Develop orbital prediction and collision warning algorithms
- Apply AI/ML technology to object identification and anomalous behavior detection
- Establish SSA data-sharing mechanisms with friendly nations

### Security Value of SSA

| Capability | Security Contribution |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Collision warning | Protect indigenous satellites from debris impacts |
| Proximity detection | Detect suspicious approach behavior toward indigenous satellites |
| Launch detection | Detect space launch activities in the region |
| Threat assessment | Analyze the deployment and capabilities of potential ASAT threats |
| Attribution capability | Determine the responsible party when space incidents occur |

## 8.5 Integration with Communications Resilience

### Cross-Chapter Linkage

Space capabilities and communications resilience (Chapter 5) are directly and structurally linked. Space-based communications constitute a critical layer within the national multi-layer communications resilience architecture, while communications requirements in turn serve as an important driver for space capability development.

### Integration Architecture

**Position of Indigenous Satellite Communications in the Multi-Layer Architecture**

| Communications Layer | Technology | Role of Space Capabilities |
|---------------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Layer 1: Submarine cables | Fiber optic | No direct relationship |
| Layer 2: Commercial LEO satellites | Starlink, etc. | Dependent on foreign commercial systems |
| Layer 2.5: Indigenous satellite communications | Domestically produced communications satellites | Autonomous, sovereign space communications |
| Layer 3: HF/UHF radio | Traditional radio | No direct relationship |
| Layer 4: LoRa/Mesh | Community networks | No direct relationship |

The value of indigenous satellite communications lies in filling the capability gap between "commercial LEO satellites" and "traditional radio":

- Not subject to the terms of service of foreign commercial providers
- Can be customized to Taiwan's specific requirements (coverage area, encryption standards, bandwidth allocation)
- Government and military retain full control over system operations and information security

### Technical Integration Approaches

**Ground Segment Integration**

1. **Unified ground control architecture**: Integrate ground control of indigenous satellites with the communications resilience command center
2. **Multi-mode terminal equipment**: Develop terminals capable of simultaneously receiving signals from commercial LEO satellites and indigenous satellites
3. **Automatic switchover mechanism**: When commercial satellite service is interrupted, automatically switch to indigenous satellite communications

**Space Segment Integration**

1. **Inter-satellite links**: Establish optical or RF inter-satellite links among indigenous satellites to reduce dependence on ground relay stations
2. **Signal relay**: Indigenous communications satellites can relay data for remote sensing satellites, improving intelligence transmission timeliness
3. **Spectrum management**: Unified frequency planning across all satellite types to avoid interference

### International Cooperation Dimensions

The development of indigenous space capabilities does not preclude international cooperation; indeed, partnerships should be actively pursued to accelerate development:

| Cooperation Area | Potential Partners | Cooperation Model |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Satellite component supply | United States, Europe, Japan | Commercial procurement, technology licensing |
| Launch services | SpaceX, Arianespace, ISRO | Commercial launch contracts |
| SSA data sharing | United States, Japan, Australia | Government-to-government agreements |
| Joint R&D | Japan (JAXA), South Korea (KARI) | Research collaboration |
| Talent development | International space agencies | Researcher exchanges |
| Ground station networks | Friendly nations | Reciprocal ground station hosting |

## 8.6 Policy Recommendations

**Short-Term Measures (1-2 Years)**

1. **Space security white paper**: Under the leadership of the National Security Council, formulate Taiwan's first space security white paper, clearly defining the role and development priorities of space capabilities within the national security framework.
2. **SSA baseline infrastructure**: Deploy the first tranche of space situational awareness optical facilities and establish basic orbital object tracking capability.
3. **Small satellite technology validation**: Accelerate development and launch of technology demonstration satellites to validate critical technologies.
4. **Space talent cultivation**: Expand space technology academic programs and industry-academia partnerships, cultivating talent in satellite design, orbital mechanics, space communications, and related fields.
5. **Commercial satellite communications procurement**: Enter into government-level service agreements with multiple LEO satellite communications operators to meet near-term communications resilience requirements.

**Medium-Term Measures (3-5 Years)**

6. **Initial optical remote sensing constellation deployment**: Complete the deployment and commissioning of the first batch of optical remote sensing satellites.
7. **SAR satellite first launch**: Launch the first synthetic aperture radar satellite and establish all-weather monitoring capability.
8. **SSA center establishment**: Establish a Space Situational Awareness Center integrating domestic sensor data and internationally shared information.
9. **Space communications prototype system**: Develop and validate an indigenous satellite communications system prototype.
10. **Space industry chain cultivation**: Through government procurement and R&D subsidies, nurture a domestic supply chain for space components and subsystems.

**Long-Term Measures (5-10 Years)**

11. **Full constellation deployment**: Complete deployment of satellite constellations across all four clusters — optical, SAR, ELINT, and communications.
12. **Space communications integration**: Formally integrate indigenous satellite communications into the national multi-layer communications resilience architecture.
13. **Launch capability assessment**: Evaluate the feasibility and cost-effectiveness of developing a small launch vehicle to reduce dependence on foreign launch services.
14. **Space traffic management**: Develop space traffic management capabilities and participate in the formulation of international space governance rules.
15. **Next-generation space technologies**: Invest in R&D of next-generation space technologies including space debris removal, on-orbit servicing, and in-space manufacturing.
