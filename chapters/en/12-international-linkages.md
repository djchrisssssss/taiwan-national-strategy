# Chapter 12: International Linkages — From Seeking Protection to Building Indispensability

> **Core Thesis**: Taiwan's international strategy should shift from passively seeking external protection to actively building indispensability, leveraging technological interdependence, intelligence cooperation, and democratic institutional values to ensure that major international actors have a direct stake in Taiwan's stability and survival.

---

## 12.1 From Seeking Protection to Indispensability

### The Limitations of the Traditional Security Paradigm

Taiwan's international security strategy has long been built on two core assumptions:

1. **External protection assumption**: Based on the Taiwan Relations Act and strategic interests, the United States will provide security guarantees when Taiwan faces military threats.
2. **Status quo maintenance assumption**: As long as Taiwan does not take actions that alter the status quo, the international community will continue to support peace and stability across the Taiwan Strait.

These two assumptions are not without basis, but as the sole foundation for a national security strategy, the risks are rising:

| Risk Factor | Description | Trend |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| U.S.-China power transition | Whether the United States has the will and capacity to sustain its Taiwan Strait security commitment amid shifting global power structures | Increasing uncertainty |
| Erosion of strategic ambiguity | Strategic ambiguity faces growing pressure in an era of information transparency | Persistent strain |
| Ally fatigue | Multiple global crises (Ukraine, Middle East) divert the attention and resources of the U.S. and its allies | Intensifying competition |
| Shifting economic dependence | Deep economic connections between major countries and China may constrain their security commitments | Structural persistence |
| Domestic political variables | Domestic political changes in allied nations may affect the stability of their Taiwan policies | Increasing volatility |

### The Indispensability Strategy

The core logic of the indispensability strategy is to position Taiwan in roles within the international system that cannot be easily replaced, thereby directly linking the interests of major international actors to Taiwan's stability. This does not replace security alliances but rather builds deeper structural foundations for them.

Indispensability can be constructed across multiple dimensions:

**Technological Indispensability**: Taiwan's leadership in advanced semiconductor manufacturing makes the global technology industry directly dependent on Taiwan's stability. This is currently the most prominent source of indispensability.

**Intelligence Indispensability**: Taiwan's geographic position and linguistic-cultural capabilities give it advantages in certain intelligence domains that other countries cannot easily replicate.

**Institutional Indispensability**: As a successful model of democratic governance in a Chinese-speaking society, Taiwan's survival carries institutional significance that transcends geopolitics.

**Supply Chain Indispensability**: Beyond semiconductors, Taiwan's role in the supply chains of multiple precision manufacturing sectors also carries a degree of indispensability.

## 12.2 Technological Interdependence Networks

### Semiconductors: The Existing Strategic Lever

Taiwan's position in the global semiconductor industry is widely known, but its strategic implications warrant deeper analysis:

| Indicator | Figure | Strategic Implication |
|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| Global market share in advanced processes (sub-7nm) | Approximately 90% | Global AI, 5G, and high-performance computing depend on Taiwan's production |
| Global foundry market share | Approximately 60% | All major technology companies are customers |
| Advanced packaging technology leadership | CoWoS, InFO, etc. | Bottleneck capability for AI chip packaging |
| Industrial cluster effect | Complete upstream and downstream supply chain | Cannot be replicated elsewhere in the short term |

TSMC's client list spans the world's most prominent technology companies — Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, Broadcom, and others. This means that a disruption to Taiwan's semiconductor production capacity would directly impact the core technology industries of the United States, Europe, Japan, and South Korea.

However, the "Silicon Shield" strategy is not without risk:

1. **Diversification trend**: The U.S. CHIPS Act, the European Chips Act, and Japan's semiconductor investment programs reflect the intent of major economies to reduce their dependence on Taiwan.
2. **Technological catch-up**: Samsung and Intel continue to invest in advanced process R&D. While they are unlikely to close the gap with TSMC in the short term, the long-term differential may narrow.
3. **Wartime vulnerability**: Semiconductor manufacturing facilities are highly concentrated and extraordinarily precise, making them vulnerable to destruction in a military conflict. The deterrent effect of the "Silicon Shield" rests on a paradox — if conflict actually occurs, the shield itself may be destroyed.

### Strategies for Deepening Technological Interdependence

Taiwan should not rely solely on its existing semiconductor advantage but should proactively expand the depth and breadth of technological interdependence:

**Advanced Packaging and Heterogeneous Integration**
- In the post-Moore's Law era, the importance of advanced packaging technology continues to grow. Taiwan holds a leading position in technologies such as CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate) and should sustain investment to maintain this advantage.
- Heterogeneous integration (combining dies of different materials and processes within a single package) represents the next frontier of semiconductor technology; Taiwan should seize the initiative.

**Semiconductor Materials and Equipment**
- Taiwan still has room to improve its self-sufficiency in semiconductor materials (photoresists, specialty chemicals) and equipment. Building capabilities in these areas can further deepen supply chain interdependence.

**Next-Generation Technology Positioning**
- Quantum computing chips, photonic computing, neuromorphic computing, and other next-generation technologies are still in early stages. Taiwan should leverage its existing semiconductor manufacturing base to establish footholds in these domains.
- Collaboration with international research institutions can accelerate technology positioning while building new interdependency relationships.

**Joint R&D Mechanisms**
- Establish government-to-government joint semiconductor R&D programs with the United States, Japan, and the EU, creating mutual dependencies in technology development.
- For example: Taiwan-Japan cooperation in materials R&D, Taiwan-U.S. cooperation in advanced processes, and Taiwan-EU cooperation in automotive and industrial semiconductors.

## 12.3 Intelligence and Security Cooperation

### Taiwan's Intelligence Value

Taiwan possesses structural advantages in specific intelligence domains that make it a valuable security cooperation partner:

**Geographic Position**
- Taiwan occupies a critical position in the First Island Chain, and its electronic surveillance capabilities can cover military activities along China's southeastern coast.
- The Taiwan Strait is one of the world's busiest shipping lanes; Taiwan's monitoring data for this area carries strategic value.
- Taiwan's geographic position also gives it potential contributions to Space Situational Awareness (SSA).

**Linguistic and Cultural Intelligence Capabilities**
- Taiwan possesses a large pool of intelligence analysts who are native Mandarin speakers and understand the nuances of Chinese political culture.
- Its capacity to analyze China's internal political dynamics, evolving military doctrine, and social trends is something that Western intelligence agencies cannot fully replicate internally.
- Taiwan's academic community of China studies scholars is among the most in-depth globally.

**Cyber Threat Intelligence**
- Taiwan has long been on the front line of cyberattacks originating from China, accumulating rich threat intelligence and offensive-defensive experience.
- This experience has direct reference value for other countries facing similar threats.

### Intelligence Cooperation Framework

Taiwan's intelligence cooperation should be structured across multiple tiers:

| Cooperation Tier | Content | Partners | Basis for Reciprocity |
|-----------------|---------|----------|----------------------|
| Strategic intelligence | Military deployment assessment, intent analysis | United States, Japan | Geographic position and analytical capability |
| Technical intelligence | Electronic signals intelligence, cyber threat intelligence | Five Eyes members (indirect) | Detection capabilities and frontline experience |
| Open-source intelligence | Analysis of China's internal dynamics | Broad cooperation among democracies | Linguistic and cultural analytical capability |
| Cybersecurity | Incident reporting, threat indicator sharing | International cybersecurity community | Attack samples and offensive-defensive experience |

### Pathways for Deepening Security Cooperation

**Military Interoperability**
- Without engaging in formal military alliance frameworks, enhance interoperability between Taiwan's armed forces and those of friendly nations in communications, operational procedures, and logistics.
- Joint exercises and training (even if conducted with a low profile) can dramatically improve combined operational effectiveness at critical moments.

**Security Dialogue Mechanisms**
- Expand and deepen the level and frequency of bilateral security dialogues — Taiwan-U.S., Taiwan-Japan, Taiwan-Australia, and others.
- Explore the possibility of establishing security dialogues with European nations (particularly the United Kingdom, France, and Germany).

**Non-Traditional Security Cooperation**
- Expand international cooperation in non-traditional security domains such as maritime search and rescue, disaster relief, counter-terrorism, and counter-piracy. Cooperation in these areas is less politically sensitive but builds practical cooperative habits and mutual trust.

## 12.4 The Strategic Value of Democratic Institutions

### The Institutional Demonstration Effect

As the most mature democratic system in a Chinese-speaking society, Taiwan's very survival carries significant institutional meaning:

- **Proof of democratic viability**: Taiwan's democratic experience refutes the argument that "Chinese societies are unsuited to democracy." This holds profound implications for discussions of political development within China, which is precisely why Taiwan's democratic system itself is perceived as a threat by certain actors.
- **A model of peaceful transition**: Taiwan's peaceful transformation from an authoritarian system to a democratic society provides a reference model for other nations in transition.
- **The resilience of free societies**: Taiwan's ability to maintain an open society and democratic governance while continuously facing external security threats demonstrates the resilience of democratic systems.

### The Role of Democratic Values in the International System

Within the broader framework of global competition between democratic and authoritarian systems, Taiwan's democratic institutions carry strategic value that transcends bilateral relationships:

**Ideological Dimension**
- Democratic nations tend to support the survival of democratic systems. Taiwan's democratic status gives it a natural foundation of sympathy in the public opinion of democratic countries.
- This sympathy translates into political pressure, compelling democratic leaders to respond when facing a Taiwan Strait crisis.

**Normative Dimension**
- Using force to alter Taiwan's status quo would constitute a challenge to the rules-based international order. Supporting Taiwan's stability is thus connected to the broader interest in maintaining international order.
- Taiwan's performance on governance indicators such as human rights, rule of law, and anti-corruption reinforces the international community's perception of Taiwan as a partner "worthy of support."

**Practical Dimension**
- The transparency and rule-of-law foundation of democratic institutions make Taiwan a trustworthy business partner and technology cooperation partner.
- Governance qualities such as intellectual property protection, contract enforcement, and regulatory transparency are prerequisites for deep technology cooperation.

### Strengthening Democratic Resilience

Taiwan's democratic system itself requires continuous strengthening to maintain its value as a strategic asset:

- **Electoral integrity**: Ensure the transparency and fairness of the electoral process, guarding against external interference.
- **Press freedom and media quality**: While safeguarding press freedom, elevate media verification standards and public credibility.
- **Depth of civic engagement**: Move beyond electoral democracy to develop participatory democratic mechanisms (such as citizens' assemblies and participatory budgeting) that deepen democracy's social foundations.
- **Anti-corruption and good governance**: Continuously improve governance quality to sustain the international community's positive assessment of Taiwan's institutions.

## 12.5 Multilateral Engagement Strategy

### Functional International Participation

Taiwan's formal diplomatic space is constrained, but opportunities for substantive participation in functional international organizations and multilateral mechanisms remain:

| Domain | Existing Platforms | Expansion Opportunities | Taiwan's Contributions |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Public health | WHO observer (unstable) | Global Health Security partnerships | Pandemic response experience, medical technology |
| Trade and economy | WTO member, APEC | CPTPP accession, digital economy agreements | Technology supply chain, market openness |
| Climate change | Informal participation | Carbon trading mechanisms, green energy technology cooperation | Semiconductor energy-saving technology, renewable energy |
| Cybersecurity | International cybersecurity community | Budapest Convention, multilateral cybersecurity dialogues | Offensive-defensive experience, threat intelligence |
| Space | Limited participation | Civilian space cooperation programs | Satellite components, ground receiving stations |
| Standards setting | Some international standards bodies | 5G, AI, quantum technology standards | Technical expertise, industry experience |

### Emerging Multilateral Frameworks

Traditional multilateral organizations have erected barriers to Taiwan's participation due to China's influence. However, emerging multilateral frameworks present new opportunities:

**Technology Standards Alliances**
- Mechanisms such as the U.S.-EU Trade and Technology Council (TTC) are shaping future technology standards and norms. As a critical technology supplier, Taiwan has grounds for inclusion in these discussions.
- Multilateral coordination on semiconductor supply chain resilience (such as the Fab 4 — U.S., Japan, South Korea, Taiwan) provides an informal yet substantive multilateral participation platform.

**Democratic Technology Governance**
- Cooperative frameworks among democracies on AI governance, data protection, and digital rights are taking shape. Taiwan's technological capabilities and democratic institutions make it a natural participant.

**Economic Security Architectures**
- Supply chain security has been elevated from a purely economic issue to a national security issue. Taiwan's role in critical supply chains earns it a seat at the table in economic security-related multilateral dialogues.

### Deepening Bilateral Relationships

Given the constraints on multilateral participation, deepening bilateral relationships remains the main axis of Taiwan's international strategy:

**United States**: The most important security partner. Pathways for deepening include qualitative improvements in arms sales, institutionalization of economic dialogue (the U.S.-Taiwan Initiative on 21st-Century Trade), and technology cooperation (semiconductors, AI, quantum).

**Japan**: A geographically adjacent natural security partner. Pathways include elevating security dialogues to higher levels, semiconductor industry cooperation (Taiwan-Japan joint ventures), and disaster prevention and relief cooperation.

**European Union and its Member States**: Important partners for economic and technological cooperation. Pathways include investment protection agreements, science and technology cooperation frameworks, and democratic governance dialogues.

**Indo-Pacific Democracies**: Australia, India, Canada, New Zealand, and others. Pathways include trade diversification, educational exchanges, and non-traditional security cooperation.

**Central and Eastern European Countries**: The Czech Republic, Lithuania, and others have already demonstrated willingness to deepen relations with Taiwan. These relationships carry a demonstration effect that may encourage other countries to follow suit.

## 12.6 Policy Recommendations

### Strategic Level

1. **Develop a National Indispensability Strategy**: The Executive Yuan should coordinate the development of an indispensability strategy integrating technology, intelligence, institutional, and economic dimensions, replacing the traditional framework that measures success primarily by the number of diplomatic recognitions. This strategy should establish specific interdependence depth indicators and conduct regular assessments.

2. **Establish a Strategic Communications Mechanism**: Under the National Security Council's coordination, develop consistent and effective external strategic communications. Core messaging should focus on Taiwan's indispensability and reciprocal value, rather than appealing solely to sympathy or moral obligation.

3. **Early Warning and Scenario Planning**: Conduct advance scenario planning for potential changes in the international system (such as adjustments in U.S. Taiwan policy or escalation of regional conflicts) and build contingency options. Avoid being caught unprepared at critical junctures.

### Technology and Economy

4. **Expand the Depth and Breadth of Technological Interdependence**: Beyond semiconductors, actively build international cooperation and interdependence in AI, quantum technology, biotechnology, and green energy technology. Each new link of technological interdependence is another thread in the safety net.

5. **Advance CPTPP Accession**: Continue to push forward the process of joining the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP). CPTPP membership holds not only economic value but also the strategic significance of institutional linkage.

6. **Establish a Technology Diplomacy Mechanism**: Create a dedicated technology diplomacy unit within the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, leveraging Taiwan's technological capabilities to advance international relations. Technology cooperation programs can serve as entry points for establishing new bilateral relationships.

### Security and Intelligence

7. **Institutionalize Deeper Intelligence Cooperation**: Elevate existing intelligence cooperation from an ad hoc basis to institutional frameworks, establishing regular exchange mechanisms, shared databases, and joint analytical capabilities.

8. **Strengthen Military Interoperability**: To the extent feasible, enhance interoperability with the armed forces of friendly nations. Improvements in communications system compatibility, shared operational procedures, and mutual logistics support capabilities are critically valuable at moments of crisis.

### Institutions and Values

9. **Invest in Democratic Governance Quality**: Continuously improve Taiwan's governance quality, particularly in rule of law, transparency, anti-corruption, and human rights. These indicators directly influence the international community's willingness to regard Taiwan as a "partner worthy of support."

10. **Advance International Democratic Dialogue**: Proactively host and participate in international dialogues and research on democratic governance, encompassing emerging issues such as digital democracy, AI governance, and climate justice. Taiwan should not merely be a beneficiary of democracy but should become a contributor to democratic innovation.
