# Chapter 6: Financial Resilience — Sanctions Defense, Digital Assets, and Blockchain Backup

> **Key Thesis**: Financial systems have become weaponized instruments in modern conflict. Taiwan must build resilience against financial isolation and sanctions pressure through foreign exchange reserve diversification, digital asset backup channels, and blockchain-based preservation of critical data.

---

## 6.1 Financial Warfare and the Weaponization of Sanctions

### Background

Financial sanctions have become a central instrument of international conflict in the twenty-first century. Unlike conventional military action, financial warfare can deliver systemic shocks without the use of physical force, and its effects often materialize within hours. Financial security has consequently been elevated from an economic policy concern to a core component of national security.

The financial sanctions imposed on Russia by Western nations following the 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict provide the most extensive contemporary case study:

| Sanctions Measure | Scope of Impact | Time Scale |
|-------------------|----------------|------------|
| SWIFT system disconnection | International payment and settlement disruption | Effective within days |
| Central bank foreign reserve freezes | ~$300 billion in assets rendered inaccessible | Immediate effect |
| Cross-border transaction restrictions | Trade financing for imports/exports impeded | Full impact within weeks |
| Energy transaction restrictions | Oil and natural gas revenue channels constrained | Gradual effect |
| Individual and entity sanctions | Oligarch and state-owned enterprise asset freezes | Immediate effect |

### Taiwan Scenario Analysis

The financial threat scenarios facing Taiwan differ structurally from the Russian case:

**Potential Financial Pressure Scenarios for Taiwan**

1. **Passive isolation**: Not triggered by Taiwan's own actions, but rather by regional conflict prompting international financial institutions to proactively reduce their Taiwan exposure on compliance grounds.
2. **Elevated counterparty risk**: Geopolitical tensions cause international banks to charge higher risk premiums on Taiwan-related transactions or to reduce credit lines.
3. **Capital flight pressure**: Crisis expectations trigger accelerated withdrawal of foreign capital from Taiwan's capital markets and banking system.
4. **Payment system disruption**: Cross-border payment channels are interrupted through cyberattacks or sanctions pressure.
5. **Trade finance collapse under blockade**: A maritime blockade renders imports and exports impossible, removing the operational basis of the trade finance system.

The scale of Taiwan's financial system is significant. According to recent statistics, Taiwan's foreign exchange reserves exceed US$560 billion (ranking among the top five globally), total banking system assets surpass NT$60 trillion, and securities market capitalization exceeds NT$50 trillion. These figures indicate that any shock to the financial system would have profoundly far-reaching consequences.

## 6.2 Foreign Exchange Reserve Diversification

### Current Assessment

The foreign exchange reserves managed by the Central Bank of the Republic of China (Taiwan) constitute the first line of defense for national financial security. However, the effectiveness of these reserves depends on their composition and custodial arrangements:

**Key Issues**

1. **Currency concentration**: Central bank foreign exchange reserves globally are predominantly denominated in US dollar assets (approximately 58-60%), and Taiwan is no exception. Excessive concentration in a single currency system means that policy decisions by the issuing country can directly affect the accessibility of reserves.
2. **Custodial location risk**: Foreign exchange reserves are largely held as financial assets — bonds, deposits, and the like — at overseas financial institutions. Under extreme scenarios, these assets could be frozen by jurisdictional decisions of the countries in which they are held. Approximately half of Russia's central bank reserves were frozen because they were held in Western countries.
3. **Liquidity management**: Reserve assets must balance safety, liquidity, and yield, but liquidity demand surges dramatically during crises.

### Diversification Strategy

| Strategy Dimension | Current State | Recommended Direction | Considerations |
|-------------------|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| Currency diversification | Predominantly USD | Moderately increase holdings in EUR, JPY, SGD | Liquidity and exchange rate volatility risk |
| Physical assets | Low proportion of gold reserves | Gradually increase gold holdings | Storage location and liquidity |
| Custodial locations | Concentrated in major financial centers | Distribute across multiple jurisdictions | Counterparty risk management |
| Asset classes | Primarily government bonds | Moderate allocation to highly liquid alternative assets | Credit risk and market risk |

Gold possesses unique value under extreme scenarios: it does not depend on any single country's financial system, carries no counterparty risk, and has historically maintained purchasing power during geopolitical conflicts. Taiwan's gold reserves as a proportion of total foreign exchange reserves have been persistently low (approximately 4-5%), in stark contrast to Germany (~70%) and the United States (~78%). A moderate increase in gold holdings is a pragmatic option for enhancing reserve resilience.

## 6.3 Digital Assets as a Backup Instrument

### Background

The role of cryptocurrencies and digital assets in financial resilience frameworks has moved beyond the theoretical stage. Multiple international cases demonstrate that digital assets have provided alternative channels for value storage and transfer in scenarios where traditional financial infrastructure was damaged or unavailable.

**International Cases**

- **Ukraine (2022-present)**: The government publicly accepted cryptocurrency donations, receiving a cumulative total exceeding US$200 million. In areas where the banking system was disrupted by war, cryptocurrencies served as a fund transfer instrument for some citizens.
- **Afghanistan (2021)**: After the Taliban takeover froze the banking system, some NGOs maintained humanitarian aid fund flows through cryptocurrency channels.
- **Venezuela**: Under economic sanctions and hyperinflation, Bitcoin and stablecoins became alternative tools for civilian wealth preservation and cross-border remittances.

### The Role of Digital Assets in Taiwan's Financial Resilience

Digital assets are not intended to replace the traditional financial system, but rather to serve as backup channels under extreme scenarios. Their positioning should encompass the following dimensions:

**Government Level**

1. **Strategic reserve supplement**: Allocate a minimal proportion of mainstream cryptocurrencies (such as Bitcoin) within foreign exchange reserves as a store of value that cannot be frozen by foreign jurisdictions.
2. **Cross-border payment backup**: Establish the technical capability and operational procedures to conduct emergency cross-border payments via blockchain networks.
3. **International aid reception channel**: Pre-establish the technical infrastructure and legal framework for receiving international aid in cryptocurrency form.

**Civilian Level**

4. **Financial education**: Incorporate basic digital asset knowledge into financial literacy education, equipping citizens with the ability to use alternative financial tools in emergency situations.
5. **Stablecoin applications**: Study the potential application of USD-pegged stablecoins in domestic payment scenarios as a backup for traditional payment systems.

**Risk Management**

Digital asset backup plans require careful management of the following risks:

| Risk Type | Description | Mitigation Measures |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Price volatility | Extreme price fluctuations in cryptocurrencies | Prioritize stablecoins; limit volatile asset proportion |
| Technical risk | Private key management, smart contract vulnerabilities | Multi-signature wallets, cold storage, independent audits |
| Regulatory risk | Tightening international cryptocurrency regulation | Compliance framework design, multi-chain deployment |
| Liquidity risk | Market liquidity evaporation under extreme scenarios | Diversify across multiple exchanges and decentralized platforms |
| Operational risk | Personnel errors or insider threats | Standard operating procedures, separation of duties |

## 6.4 Blockchain and Critical Data Preservation

### Problem Statement

The functioning of a modern state is highly dependent on digitized critical data, including but not limited to:

- Land registration and real property ownership records
- Household registration and identity verification data
- Financial account and transaction records
- Business registration and commercial records
- Intellectual property registrations
- Medical records

This data is currently stored primarily in centralized database systems. Even with backup mechanisms in place, data centers may be simultaneously disabled by physical attack, cyberattack, or power outage. In a protracted conflict scenario, the loss of critical data would severely impair post-war reconstruction and the restoration of social order.

### Application of Blockchain Technology

Distributed ledger technology (DLT) can provide an additional preservation layer for critical data. Its core advantages include:

1. **Distributed storage**: Data replicas are distributed across multiple nodes worldwide; infrastructure destruction in a single geographic area does not result in data loss.
2. **Immutability**: The cryptographic structure of blockchains ensures that recorded data cannot be retroactively altered.
3. **Verifiability**: Anyone can independently verify the integrity and authenticity of the data.
4. **Disintermediation**: No dependency on the continued operation of any single institution or server.

**Recommended Application Architecture**

| Data Type | Blockchain Approach | Storage Method | Privacy Considerations |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Land registration | Public chain + sidechain | Hash on-chain; original data encrypted off-chain | Ownership public, details encrypted |
| Identity data | Permissioned chain | Decentralized Identity (DID) standards | Zero-knowledge proof verification |
| Financial records | Hybrid architecture | Summary on-chain; full data encrypted and distributed | Strict encryption and access control |
| Business registration | Public chain | Registration facts on-chain | Primarily public information |

**Implementation Considerations**

- Privacy protection: Critical personal data should not be stored in plaintext on public blockchains; encryption, hashing, or zero-knowledge proof techniques should be employed.
- Legal effect: Relevant legislation must be amended to confer legal effect on data records maintained on blockchains.
- Data update mechanisms: Maintainable update mechanisms must be designed to reflect data changes (e.g., land transfers).
- International cooperation: Collaborate with friendly nations to maintain blockchain nodes within their territories, increasing the geographic diversity of data survivability.

## 6.5 Central Bank Digital Currency and Payment Resilience

### Background

A Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) is a digital form of fiat currency issued by a central bank. The Central Bank of the Republic of China (Taiwan) has conducted CBDC research and pilot projects but has not yet formally issued one.

In the context of financial resilience, the value of a CBDC lies not only in improved payment efficiency but also in the potential to maintain basic transaction capability when traditional payment infrastructure is damaged.

### Current Assessment

Global CBDC development status:

| Country/Region | Stage | Technical Architecture | Offline Payment Capability |
|---------------|-------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| China (Digital RMB) | Large-scale pilot | Two-tier architecture | Supported |
| Eurozone (Digital Euro) | Preparatory phase | Under deliberation | Planned |
| Japan (Digital Yen) | Pilot | Under deliberation | Under deliberation |
| Taiwan | Research and proof of concept | Under deliberation | Under deliberation |
| Bahamas (Sand Dollar) | Issued | Two-tier architecture | Supported |

### Payment Resilience Analysis

Taiwan's existing payment infrastructure comprises:

1. **Cash**: Physical currency remains usable when networks and power are disrupted, but is inconvenient for large transactions and carries security risks.
2. **Bank transfers**: Dependent on interbank settlement systems (e.g., the Financial Information Service Company's interbank system); system outages disrupt transfer capability.
3. **Credit/debit cards**: Dependent on POS terminals, network connectivity, and clearing systems.
4. **Mobile payments**: Dependent on smartphones, mobile networks, and back-end systems.

Under scenarios involving large-scale infrastructure damage, all of the above electronic payment channels could fail simultaneously. If a CBDC is designed to support offline peer-to-peer transactions (e.g., via NFC or Bluetooth), it can maintain basic transaction capability when networks and banking systems are disrupted.

**CBDC Resilience Design Elements**

1. **Offline transaction capability**: Direct device-to-device transfers without network connectivity
2. **Tiered limit management**: Offline transactions subject to amount ceilings to reduce risk
3. **Deferred settlement mechanism**: Offline transactions undergo final settlement once network connectivity is restored
4. **Hardware security modules**: Secure chips to prevent double-spending in offline transactions
5. **Multi-carrier support**: Not limited to smartphones; also supports smart cards and other low-power devices

## 6.6 Policy Recommendations

**Short-Term Measures (1-2 Years)**

1. **Financial stress scenario exercises**: The Central Bank and the Financial Supervisory Commission should jointly conduct stress tests simulating financial sanctions and isolation scenarios, assessing the financial system's survivability under various conditions.
2. **Foreign exchange reserve review**: Review the currency composition, custodial location distribution, and asset class allocation of reserves, and propose diversification adjustments.
3. **Gold reserve augmentation**: Launch a phased program to increase gold reserves, with a medium-term target of raising the gold share to 10-15%.
4. **Cryptocurrency regulatory framework**: Complete the virtual asset management regulatory framework to establish the legal basis for legitimate use of digital assets as backup financial instruments.

**Medium-Term Measures (3-5 Years)**

5. **CBDC offline payment development**: Designate offline transaction capability as a core requirement for Taiwan's CBDC design, and prioritize its development and testing.
6. **Critical data blockchain backup**: Launch a pilot program for blockchain-based backup of land registration and identity data.
7. **International payment backup channels**: Establish bilateral payment mechanisms with friendly nations that do not depend on SWIFT.
8. **Financial resilience exercises**: Conduct regular cross-institutional financial resilience exercises, including scenarios such as payment system outages and foreign exchange market anomalies.

**Long-Term Measures (5-10 Years)**

9. **Decentralized financial infrastructure**: Build a backup financial infrastructure architecture that does not depend on any single centralized system.
10. **Blockchain public services platform**: Establish a national-level blockchain platform providing public services including critical data preservation, identity verification, and asset registration.
11. **Financial sovereignty technical capability**: Cultivate fintech and cryptography talent to ensure national autonomy in financial technology domains.
12. **International financial resilience alliance**: Establish financial resilience cooperation mechanisms with like-minded nations, including cross-border payment backup, foreign exchange swap agreements, and data backup cooperation.
