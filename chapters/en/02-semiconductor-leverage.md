# Chapter 2: Semiconductor Leverage — Advanced Nodes, Supply Chain Irreplaceability, and Strategic Interdependence

> **Central Thesis**: Taiwan's dominant position in advanced semiconductor manufacturing constitutes one of the most powerful economic-strategic levers of the modern era. However, this advantage is accompanied by structural risks including geographic concentration, talent bottlenecks, and equipment dependency. The strategic objective is to maintain technological leadership while converting industrial advantage into deep international interdependency networks, ensuring that any action threatening Taiwan's security carries incalculable global economic costs.

---

## 2.1 Position in the Global Semiconductor Industry

### Background

Semiconductors are the foundational material of the digital age. From smartphones to artificial intelligence servers, from electric vehicle control systems to precision weapons guidance modules, virtually every modern technology system depends on chip computing power. The semiconductor industry is therefore not merely an industrial sector but the underlying infrastructure of the global technology order.

Taiwan's position within this industry is historically unique. Since the founding of TSMC in 1987, Taiwan has progressively built the world's most complete semiconductor foundry ecosystem. This ecosystem extends beyond TSMC itself to encompass packaging and testing (e.g., ASE Technology), IC design (e.g., MediaTek), photomask manufacturing, chemical materials supply, and other upstream and downstream segments. The depth and density of this entire industrial chain are unmatched anywhere in the world.

The strategic importance of Taiwan's semiconductor industry can be understood through the following data:

| Indicator | Estimated Value | Source |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| Global foundry market share | >60% | Annual industry research reports |
| Advanced node (sub-7nm) global share | >90% | Public industry analysis |
| TSMC global revenue share (foundry market) | ~55% | Company financial reports |
| Semiconductors as share of Taiwan's total exports | ~35-40% | Ministry of Finance statistics |
| Direct employment | >300,000 persons | Ministry of Economic Affairs statistics |

### Current Assessment

As of the time of this analysis, TSMC has entered volume production on its 3-nanometer process and is actively advancing research and development on 2-nanometer and more advanced nodes. Each successive process generation represents not only technological progress but also a continued elevation of barriers to entry. Advanced node R&D requires astronomical capital investment (construction costs for a single advanced fab now exceed $20 billion), highly specialized talent teams, and decades of accumulated process know-how.

Samsung Electronics and Intel are currently the only two companies attempting to close the gap with TSMC in advanced nodes. Both, however, face significant yield improvement challenges. Samsung's 3-nanometer GAA (Gate-All-Around) process had initial volume production yields well below those of TSMC's FinFET process. Intel's "IDM 2.0" strategy, while ambitious, continues to face multiple obstacles at the execution level.

This means that for the foreseeable future (at least 5-7 years), global technology industry dependence on Taiwan's advanced chip manufacturing capabilities will persist and may even deepen. The explosive growth of artificial intelligence has further reinforced this dependence — the high-performance GPUs and AI accelerators required for AI training and inference are nearly all manufactured by TSMC.

### Analysis

The strategic implications of Taiwan's semiconductor industry can be understood through the "Silicon Shield" conceptual framework. This concept was first articulated by analyst Craig Addison in 2001, with the central argument that the global irreplaceability of Taiwan's semiconductor industry constitutes an implicit security guarantee — any attack on Taiwan would disrupt the global chip supply, with resulting economic losses far exceeding the costs of the military conflict itself.

This argument has considerable analytical foundation, but also carries important limitations and risks:

**Conditions for Silicon Shield effectiveness.** The deterrent effect of the Silicon Shield rests on two premises: (1) the global community understands and recognizes the depth of this dependency; and (2) economic costs carry sufficient weight in the adversary's decision calculus. If the adversary views territorial unification as a core interest that transcends economic considerations, the Silicon Shield's deterrent effect may be overestimated.

**Time-decay risk.** As the United States, EU, Japan, and South Korea each pursue semiconductor manufacturing reshoring or regionalization, Taiwan's market monopoly position may gradually erode over the long term. The U.S. CHIPS Act, the European Chips Act, and Japan's semiconductor revival plans all explicitly include reducing dependence on Taiwan as one of their objectives. While transfer of advanced nodes is infeasible in the near term, the long-term trend warrants vigilance.

**"Destructive use" scenario.** The most extreme scenario involves the adversary deliberately destroying Taiwan's semiconductor facilities during military operations to eliminate the asset's value to other nations. In this scenario, the Silicon Shield not only fails but becomes a target. While this scenario is less likely (because the adversary itself is heavily dependent on Taiwanese chips), it cannot be entirely ruled out.

## 2.2 Structural Risk: Geographic Concentration

### Background

The geographic concentration of Taiwan's semiconductor industry is its most prominent structural risk. The world's most advanced chip manufacturing capability is concentrated within a corridor on Taiwan's western coast roughly 200 kilometers long and 50 kilometers wide. Hsinchu Science Park is TSMC's R&D and manufacturing center; the Central Taiwan Science Park and Southern Taiwan Science Park respectively house capacity for different process generations.

This geographic concentration is not accidental but the natural result of industrial cluster effects. Semiconductor manufacturing requires highly specialized supply chains — from chemical materials to photomask manufacturing, from equipment maintenance to talent supply — and the geographic proximity of these supporting functions is key to production efficiency. From a security perspective, however, this concentration constitutes a high-value, low-redundancy target set.

### Current Assessment

| Science Park | Key Facilities | Process Capability | Strategic Risk Level |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Hsinchu Science Park | TSMC headquarters, R&D center, multiple fabs | Mature through advanced nodes | Very high |
| Central Taiwan Science Park | TSMC Central Taiwan fabs | Mid-to-advanced nodes | High |
| Southern Taiwan Science Park | TSMC Southern fabs (including most advanced nodes) | Most advanced nodes (3nm, 2nm) | Very high |
| Kaohsiung | TSMC Kaohsiung fab (under construction) | Advanced nodes | High |

Advances in precision strike capabilities have significantly elevated the risks of geographic concentration. The circular error probable (CEP) of modern cruise missiles and ballistic missiles has narrowed to the single-digit meter range. While the cleanrooms of wafer fabs possess a degree of structural robustness, the ultra-precision manufacturing equipment within them (such as EUV lithography tools) is extraordinarily sensitive to vibration and particulate contamination. Even without a direct hit on the building structure, the blast wave and airborne debris from a nearby explosion could render advanced processes inoperable for an extended period.

Furthermore, seismic risk cannot be overlooked. Taiwan sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire, and the 1999 Chi-Chi earthquake caused multiple fabs in the Hsinchu park to halt production for several weeks. The restart procedures for semiconductor processes are extremely complex; even when equipment sustains no material damage, restoring full production speed requires weeks to months.

### Analysis

The core risk of geographic concentration lies in **correlated failure**. In reliability engineering, independently dispersed nodes can dramatically reduce system failure probability through redundancy design. But when all nodes are concentrated within the same geographic area, a single event — whether a military strike, a major earthquake, or an industrial accident — can simultaneously affect every node.

Mitigation strategies for this risk include:

- **Domestic dispersal**: Building backup capacity in eastern Taiwan or other less exposed regions. This approach faces challenges related to terrain, water and power supply, and transportation, but is not technically infeasible.
- **Overseas backup**: TSMC's fab construction projects in Arizona (USA), Kumamoto (Japan), and Dresden (Germany) objectively provide partial geographic diversification. However, overseas fabs typically lag 1-2 generations behind Taiwan-based facilities in process nodes, and their production capacity is limited.
- **Critical equipment protection**: Establishing physical protection measures for the most irreplaceable equipment (such as EUV lithography tools), including seismic reinforcement, blast barriers, and rapid evacuation procedures.

## 2.3 Structural Risk: Talent and Knowledge Bottlenecks

### Background

The core competitiveness of advanced semiconductor processes lies not only in equipment and capital, but in decades of accumulated process knowledge and experience. This knowledge is highly concentrated within a small number of core engineering teams and is extremely difficult to replace. The typical development cycle for a senior process engineer is 10-15 years, and the tacit knowledge they possess is difficult to fully transfer through documentation or training programs.

### Current Assessment

Taiwan's semiconductor industry faces multiple talent challenges:

**Talent supply pressure.** Taiwan's universities produce approximately 80,000 to 100,000 STEM graduates annually, but the semiconductor industry's expansion rate consistently outpaces talent supply growth. Wage competition has extended from the domestic market to the international arena — semiconductor companies in China, the United States, and Japan all offer premium salaries to attract Taiwanese engineers, creating sustained brain-drain pressure.

**Knowledge concentration risk.** Critical knowledge pertaining to advanced processes — including EUV lithography exposure strategies, core parameters for yield improvement, and key process integration techniques — is concentrated among several hundred to several thousand core engineers. The security and retention of this cohort directly determines the continued competitiveness of Taiwan's semiconductor industry.

**Demographic structural challenge.** Taiwan's low fertility rate (total fertility rate of approximately 0.87, among the lowest in the world) means the long-term talent pool will continue to shrink. This structural trend is difficult to reverse in the short term and requires compensation through international talent recruitment and education system reform.

| Talent Indicator | Current Status | Trend |
|-----------------|----------------|-------|
| Annual semiconductor industry talent gap | Approx. 30,000-40,000 (estimated) | Widening |
| Core process engineers (advanced nodes) | On the order of several thousand | Long training cycle, insufficient supply |
| Annual talent outflow overseas | Persistent risk | Intensifying with international competition |
| Annual university graduates in semiconductor-related fields | ~10,000-20,000/year | Increasing under policy initiatives |

### Analysis

Talent security has escalated from a purely industrial policy issue to a national security issue. In extreme scenarios (such as gray-zone operations preceding a conflict), an adversary could systematically erode Taiwan's technological advantage through targeted talent recruitment, academic cooperation infiltration, or industrial espionage. The cost of such operations is far lower than military action, but the long-term effects could be equally destructive.

A strategic response requires a multi-pronged approach: strengthening trade secret protection legislation, establishing security assessment mechanisms for core talent, increasing industry compensation competitiveness to reduce outflow incentives, expanding international talent recruitment channels, and investing in the education system to enlarge the long-term talent pool.

## 2.4 Structural Risk: Equipment and Materials Dependency

### Background

Semiconductor manufacturing is among the most equipment-intensive fields in modern industry. A single advanced fab may contain equipment valued at billions of dollars, and the supply chains for this equipment are highly concentrated among a small number of international companies. This dependency constitutes another structural risk for Taiwan's semiconductor industry.

### Current Assessment

**Extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography tools.** The Netherlands-based ASML is the sole global supplier of EUV lithography systems. Each EUV tool costs upward of $150 million, and annual production capacity is limited. TSMC is among ASML's largest customers, but this single-supplier dependency means that if ASML's supply is interrupted due to geopolitical factors (such as Dutch government export controls) or production incidents, TSMC's advanced node expansion would be directly impeded.

**Other critical equipment.** Etch equipment (Tokyo Electron, Lam Research), thin film deposition equipment (Applied Materials), and inspection equipment (KLA) and other critical process tools are similarly dominated by a small number of international companies. While multiple suppliers exist in these categories, the optimal equipment choice for specific process nodes remains limited.

**Chemical materials and specialty gases.** The supply chains for high-purity chemical materials (such as photoresists and chemical-mechanical planarization slurries) and specialty gases (such as hydrogen fluoride and silane) required for advanced processes are also concentrated. Japan holds a dominant position in several critical material categories — the 2019 Japan-South Korea trade dispute, in which Japan imposed export controls on semiconductor materials to South Korea, clearly demonstrated the geopolitical vulnerability of material supply chains.

| Equipment/Material Category | Primary Suppliers | Supply Concentration | Substitution Feasibility |
|----------------------------|-------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| EUV lithography tools | ASML (Netherlands) | 100% monopoly | No short-term substitute |
| DUV lithography tools | ASML, Nikon (Japan) | Duopoly | Limited substitution |
| Etch equipment | Tokyo Electron, Lam Research, Hitachi | Oligopoly | Substitutable but requires qualification |
| EUV photoresists | JSR, Tokyo Ohka Kogyo, Shin-Etsu (Japan) | Highly concentrated | Limited substitution |
| High-purity specialty gases | Multiple international suppliers | Moderately concentrated | Substitutable; requires buildout |

### Analysis

Equipment and materials dependency means that Taiwan's semiconductor advantage is not fully autonomous. This advantage is built atop a complex international supply network, and disruption at any critical node within that network could affect Taiwan's manufacturing capabilities.

However, this dependency is bidirectional. Equipment vendors such as ASML and Applied Materials are equally dependent on TSMC as one of their largest customers. This interdependency is stable and mutually beneficial under normal conditions, but under geopolitical pressure it can become a lever in multilateral bargaining.

Strategic response directions include: establishing strategic inventories of critical equipment, developing indigenous equipment maintenance and component manufacturing capabilities, diversifying material supply sources, and building political-level supply assurance mechanisms with major equipment-supplying nations.

## 2.5 Policy Recommendations

### Short-Term (1-2 Years)

1. **Establish strategic reserves of semiconductor equipment.** For the most critical and difficult-to-replace equipment components and materials, establish 6-12 months of strategic inventory. This measure can significantly enhance short-term resilience without triggering industry alarm.

2. **Strengthen trade secret protection.** Amend the Trade Secrets Act to increase penalties for leaking core semiconductor technology. Establish an inter-ministerial technology security coordination mechanism integrating legal, intelligence, and industry resources.

3. **Launch a core talent security framework.** While respecting individual freedoms, establish a classification management mechanism for advanced node core talent, including post-departure cooling-off period regulations, strengthened non-compete provisions, and knowledge protection agreements.

### Medium-Term (3-5 Years)

4. **Promote industrial geographic dispersal.** Encourage semiconductor manufacturing facilities to disperse toward eastern or southern Taiwan, using infrastructure investment and tax incentives to reduce relocation costs. The objective is not to fully replicate existing capacity, but to ensure that critical process capabilities do not entirely overlap geographically.

5. **Deepen technology interdependency networks.** Proactively embed Taiwan's semiconductor manufacturing capabilities within the defense and technology supply chains of the United States, Japan, and the EU, giving these nations a direct economic interest in maintaining Taiwan's security. This strategy requires precise diplomatic coordination to avoid being interpreted as economic coercion.

6. **Invest in next-generation process R&D.** Ensure Taiwan maintains its lead in sub-2nm processes, advanced packaging (e.g., CoWoS, SoIC), compound semiconductors, and other next-generation technologies. Technological leadership is the foundation of all strategic leverage — once the technological edge is lost, all other strategies lose their basis.

### Long-Term (5-10 Years)

7. **Build an indigenous equipment ecosystem.** Make long-term investments in a domestic semiconductor equipment industry, with the goal not of fully replacing international suppliers but of building basic autonomous capabilities in critical areas, reducing supply disruption risk under extreme scenarios.

8. **Structural reform of the talent system.** Through education system reforms (expanding semiconductor-related degree programs, establishing industry-academia cooperative training mechanisms) and immigration policy adjustments (facilitating international semiconductor talent relocation to Taiwan), fundamentally enlarge the talent pool.

9. **"Silicon Shield 2.0" strategy.** As the global semiconductor supply chain gradually diversifies, Taiwan must think about its strategic positioning in the "post-Silicon Shield" era. Directions may include: shifting from manufacturing monopoly to leadership in technology standard-setting, establishing new forms of irreplaceability in advanced packaging, or building early positions in emerging fields such as quantum computing.

### Cross-Domain Linkages

- **Linkage with Chapter 1 (Geostrategic Position)**: The continued operation of the semiconductor industry depends on the security and stability of waters surrounding Taiwan.
- **Linkage with Chapter 3 (Asymmetric Defense)**: The survival of semiconductor facilities requires effective air defense and missile defense capabilities.
- **Linkage with Chapter 5 (Energy Resilience)**: Semiconductor manufacturing has extremely high requirements for power supply stability and quality; any power interruption could result in enormous losses.
- **Linkage with Chapter 7 (Cybersecurity)**: Semiconductor design data and process parameters are the highest-value targets for cyberattacks.
- **Linkage with Chapter 11 (Diplomacy and Alliances)**: Semiconductors are Taiwan's most powerful bargaining chip in international diplomacy.
