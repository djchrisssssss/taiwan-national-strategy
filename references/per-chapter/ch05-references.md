## Notes

- Taiwan's 97.7% energy import dependency is among the highest globally and is a critical strategic vulnerability. Virtually all fossil fuels and uranium are imported.
- LNG reserve days (7-11 days) vary seasonally. Summer peak demand reduces effective reserve duration. This is significantly shorter than comparable economies (Japan ~20 days, South Korea ~30 days).
- The petroleum reserve figure of 146 days includes both government strategic stocks and mandatory industry reserves. This is the most resilient energy reserve category.
- Offshore wind capacity figures are approximate pending final year-end reporting. The original 5.6 GW target by 2025 is behind schedule.
- TSMC's electricity consumption of ~9% of Taiwan's total (and growing) highlights the energy-semiconductor nexus. Advanced nodes (3nm, 2nm) require more energy per wafer.
- Nuclear phase-out policy remains politically contentious. Maanshan NPP units are the last operational reactors, scheduled for decommission by 2025.
- Section 5.4 (Nuclear Energy as an Energy Security Strategy Option) discusses next-generation nuclear technology purely as a strategic direction. It does not address existing Taiwan nuclear plants (Units 1-4) or make policy recommendations on the current phase-out.
- Over 80 SMR designs are tracked by the IAEA globally. Key near-term deployments include NuScale VOYGR (US), BWRX-300 (Canada), and HTR-PM (China).
- SMR fuel cycle duration (18-24 months) provides blockade resilience orders of magnitude greater than LNG reserves (7-11 days).
